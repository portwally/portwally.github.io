PROCESS

Cyanotype process

Cyanotype is one of the oldest photographic printing processes, invented in 1842 by Sir John Herschel. Using a light-sensitive solution of iron compounds and the power of UV light, each piece becomes a unique, handcrafted work of art in the iconic Prussian blue.

1

PREPARE

Mix the light-sensitive chemicals and coat paper, fabric, or other surfaces with the solution. Let dry in a dark environment.

2

COMPOSE

Place botanical elements, negatives, or objects directly onto the coated surface to create your unique composition.

3

EXPOSE

Expose the composition to sunlight or UV light. The uncovered areas will turn a rich Prussian blue while shielded areas remain white.

4

DEVELOP

Rinse in water to wash away the unexposed chemicals, revealing the final cyanotype print. The blue will deepen as it oxidizes.

THE ARTIST

Luísa Rodrigues

Based in Portugal, I've been exploring the cyanotype process for over a decade, drawn by its meditative nature and the beautiful unpredictability of each print. Working with sunlight, chemistry, and botanical specimens, I create one-of-a-kind artworks that capture the delicate beauty of the natural world in shades of Prussian blue.

My work is inspired by the intersection of science and art — the same curiosity that led Anna Atkins to create the first photographic book of botanical specimens using this very technique in 1843.
